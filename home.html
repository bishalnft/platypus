About Platypus

The platypus, sometimes referred to as the duck-billed platypus, is a semiaquatic, egg-laying mammal endemic to eastern Australia, including Tasmania. The platypus is the sole living representative or monotypic taxon of its family and genus, though a number of related species appear in the fossil record.

Habitat:

Platypus make their home in and near freshwater creeks, slow-moving rivers, lakes joined by rivers, and built water storages such as farm dams

Diet:

Platypus eat small water animals such as insect larvae, freshwater shrimps, and crayfish. The platypus, usually active at dawn and dusk, relies on its sensitive bill to find food.

Platypuses are venomous.

Why are Platypus's Important?

Not only is the platypus an important freshwater carnivore, it is an evolutionary gem, one of the last remaining monotremes—or egg-laying mammals—left on Earth. And we've only begun to discover its wealth of secrets, from life-saving antibiotics in its milk to potential diabetes cures in its venom

Why are Platypus's Endangered?

Platypus have become locally extinct in areas they once thrived due to bushfires, drought, deforestation, predation and pollution.
